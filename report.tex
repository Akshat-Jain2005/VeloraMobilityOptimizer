\section*{Executive Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Executive Summary}

This technical report documents the mid-term progress on the \textbf{Velora Mobility Optimizer}, a solution developed for the Kriti '26 Optimization Problem Statement. The problem addresses corporate employee transportation---specifically, optimizing the assignment of employee pickup requests to a heterogeneous fleet of vehicles while minimizing operational costs and respecting complex scheduling constraints.

\subsection*{Problem Classification}
The Velora problem is formally classified as a \textbf{Heterogeneous Vehicle Routing Problem with Soft Time Windows (HVRPSTW)}---an NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem \cite{toth2014,laporte2009}. Key characteristics include:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Many-to-One Structure}: Multiple employee pickup locations $\rightarrow$ single office destination
    \item \textbf{Heterogeneous Fleet}: Vehicles with varying capacities, speeds, costs, and categories
    \item \textbf{Soft Time Windows}: Priority-based delay tolerances with penalty functions \cite{taillard1997,ismail2021}
    \item \textbf{Preference Constraints}: Vehicle type preferences and sharing limits as soft constraints
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Solution Approach}
We implement a \textbf{two-phase hybrid metaheuristic}:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Multi-Start Constructive Heuristic}: Solomon I1-style insertion with priority-based ordering
    \item \textbf{Simulated Annealing Refinement}: Metropolis-based local search with relocate/exchange/2-opt neighborhoods
\end{enumerate}

% ===== ABSTRACT =====
\begin{abstract}
This technical report presents a comprehensive solution for the Velora corporate mobility optimization problem: optimally assigning employee transportation requests to a heterogeneous fleet while minimizing a weighted objective of operational cost and travel time, subject to capacity constraints, time windows, and employee preferences.

The problem is modeled as a \textbf{Heterogeneous Vehicle Routing Problem with Soft Time Windows (HVRPSTW)}---a well-studied NP-hard variant in operations research. We formulate the objective as:
\[
Z = w_c \cdot \text{CTC} + w_t \cdot \text{Time} + \sum \text{Penalties}
\]
where CTC (Cost to Company) captures distance-based vehicle operating costs, Time captures total route duration, and Penalties encode soft constraint violations.

Our algorithmic approach combines: (1) a \textbf{multi-start greedy constructive heuristic} based on Solomon's I1 insertion method \cite{solomon1987} to generate diverse feasible initial solutions, and (2) \textbf{Simulated Annealing (SA)} refinement \cite{kirkpatrick1983} using relocate, exchange, and 2-opt neighborhood operators with Metropolis acceptance criteria.

The system is implemented in \textbf{C++17} for computational efficiency, with \textbf{Python} scripts handling data parsing and report generation. Evaluation across four test cases demonstrates significant cost savings (\textbf{53.8\%--77.0\%}) compared to individual ride baselines, with sub-second computation times suitable for operational deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Context and Motivation}
Large organizations operating employee transportation services face a complex daily planning challenge: how to efficiently group employees into shared rides, assign appropriate vehicles, and sequence pickups to minimize cost while ensuring timely arrival at the workplace \cite{purba2020,aydin2025}. This problem---often called the \textit{Employee Commute Problem} or \textit{Corporate Shuttle Routing Problem}---has significant practical relevance:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cost Impact}: Employee transportation can represent 5--15\% of operational overhead for large organizations
    \item \textbf{Scale}: A typical corporate campus may process 100--500 employee requests daily across 20--50 vehicles
    \item \textbf{Complexity}: Real-world constraints include heterogeneous vehicles, employee preferences, regulatory requirements, and dynamic conditions
\end{itemize}

The Velora Mobility Optimizer addresses this problem as specified in the Kriti '26 optimization challenge, targeting the Bangalore corporate mobility context with realistic constraints and data. The Vehicle Routing Problem and its variants have been extensively studied for over fifty years \cite{laporte2009,golden2008}.

\subsection{Problem Statement Overview}
The core problem is a \textbf{Many-to-One Vehicle Routing Problem with Pickup and Delivery (VRPPD)} variant \cite{parragh2008,psaraftis1980} where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multiple employees must be picked up from distributed locations
    \item All employees are delivered to a \textit{single common destination} (office)
    \item A \textit{heterogeneous fleet} of vehicles with different capacities, types, and costs is available
    \item Employees have \textit{time windows} specifying acceptable arrival times at the office
    \item Various \textit{preferences} (vehicle type, sharing willingness) act as soft constraints
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Key Assumptions Made}
Based on the problem statement and clarifications received during the competition, we make the following modeling assumptions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Static Planning}: All employee requests are known in advance (no dynamic arrivals)
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Travel Times}: Travel times are computed from distances using fixed vehicle speeds (no real-time traffic modeling) \cite{ichoua2003}
    \item \textbf{Single Office Destination}: All employees are delivered to the same drop-off location
    \item \textbf{No Mid-Route Transfers}: An employee cannot change vehicles mid-route
    \item \textbf{No Mid-Route Drop-offs}: Employees are picked up before any drop-offs occur (standard many-to-one structure)
    \item \textbf{Service Time}: A fixed 2-minute service time is assumed at each pickup stop
    \item \textbf{Road Distance Approximation}: In the absence of a maps API, road distances are estimated as $1.4 \times$ Haversine (great-circle) distance
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Inputs, Outputs, and Constraints}

\subsubsection{Input Data}
The solver receives structured input containing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Employee Requests}: For each employee $i$:
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
        \item Pickup location (latitude, longitude)
        \item Drop-off location (common office)
        \item Time window $[E_i, L_i]$ for office arrival
        \item Priority level $P_i \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ (1 = highest)
        \item Vehicle preference (\texttt{premium}, \texttt{normal}, \texttt{any})
        \item Sharing preference (max passengers willing to share with)
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vehicle Fleet}: For each vehicle $k$:
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
        \item Capacity $Q_k$
        \item Cost per kilometer $C_k$
        \item Average speed $v_k$
        \item Vehicle type/category
        \item Start location and availability time
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration}: Objective weights, priority-based delay tolerances
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Output Data}
The solver produces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Per-vehicle routes (ordered sequence of pickup and drop-off stops)
    \item Arrival times at each stop
    \item Aggregate metrics: total distance, time, cost, and penalties
    \item Any unassigned requests (in case of hard infeasibility)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hard Constraints}
The following constraints \textbf{must never be violated}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vehicle Capacity}: $\text{Load}_k(t) \leq Q_k$ at all times
    \item \textbf{Pickup-Dropoff Precedence}: For each employee, pickup must occur before drop-off in the same route
    \item \textbf{Assignment Uniqueness}: Each employee is served by exactly one vehicle
    \item \textbf{Earliest Pickup Time ($E_i$)}: The vehicle cannot pick up employee $i$ before time $E_i$. If the vehicle arrives early, it must wait until $E_i$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Soft Constraints (Penalty-Based)}
The following constraints can be violated with associated penalties \cite{taillard1997}:

\paragraph{Time Window Handling.}
Each employee $i$ has a time window $[E_i, L_i]$ where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $E_i$ = \textbf{Earliest pickup time} --- \textbf{HARD constraint}. Cannot pick up before this time.
    \item $L_i$ = \textbf{Latest drop-off time} --- \textbf{SOFT constraint} with priority-based tolerance.
\end{itemize}

The maximum tolerable delay $f(P_i)$ is determined by the employee's priority level:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Priority} & $f(P_i)$ \textbf{(minutes)} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
\hline
$P_i = 1$ (Highest) & 5 & VIP/Executive - minimal delay tolerance \\
$P_i = 2$ & 10 & Senior staff \\
$P_i = 3$ & 15 & Standard priority \\
$P_i = 4$ & 20 & Flexible employees \\
$P_i = 5$ (Lowest) & 30 & Maximum flexibility \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Penalty Structure for Late Arrival:}
Let $t_{\text{arr}}$ be the actual arrival time at the office for employee $i$. The penalty is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\text{Penalty}_i = 
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } t_{\text{arr}} \leq L_i + f(P_i) \quad \text{(on-time or within tolerance)} \\[0.3cm]
(t_{\text{arr}} - L_i - f(P_i)) \cdot M \cdot w(P_i) & \text{if } t_{\text{arr}} > L_i + f(P_i) \quad \text{(exceeds tolerance)}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $M = 1000.0$ is the severe violation penalty multiplier
    \item $w(P_i) = (6 - P_i)$ is the priority weight (higher priority $\Rightarrow$ higher penalty)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{On-time} ($t_{\text{arr}} \leq L_i$): No penalty. Employee arrives within their requested time window.
    \item \textbf{Within tolerance} ($L_i < t_{\text{arr}} \leq L_i + f(P_i)$): \textbf{No penalty}. The delay is within the acceptable tolerance defined by the employee's priority level. This is the ``grace period'' where delays are fully acceptable.
    \item \textbf{Exceeds tolerance} ($t_{\text{arr}} > L_i + f(P_i)$): \textbf{Severe penalty}. The employee is late beyond the tolerance threshold. A large penalty is imposed proportional to the excess lateness and priority weight.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Implementation in Code:}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp]
double maxDelay = gCtx.getMaxDelay(req.priority);
double toleranceDeadline = req.lateTime + maxDelay;

if (currentTime > toleranceDeadline) {
    // Exceeds tolerance - severe penalty
    double excessLateness = currentTime - toleranceDeadline;
    eval.penaltyCost += excessLateness * M / 100.0 * getPriorityWeight(req.priority);
}
// else: within L_i or within tolerance window - NO PENALTY
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Other Soft Constraints.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vehicle Preference Mismatch}: Assigning to a non-preferred vehicle type incurs a penalty of \rupee300
    \item \textbf{Sharing Limit Violations}: Exceeding an employee's sharing preference incurs a penalty of \rupee500 per occurrence
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Optimization Objective}
The solver minimizes a composite objective function:
\begin{equation}
\min Z = w_c \cdot \text{CTC} + w_t \cdot \text{TotalTime} + \text{Penalties}
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\text{CTC} = \sum_{k} \sum_{(i,j) \in \text{route}_k} C_k \cdot d_{ij}$ is the total operating cost (Cost to Company)
    \item $\text{TotalTime} = \sum_{k} T_k$ is the total route duration across all vehicles (in minutes)
    \item $\text{Penalties} = \sum_{i} \text{Penalty}_i$ aggregates all soft constraint violations (time windows, preferences, sharing limits)
    \item $w_c = 0.7$ and $w_t = 0.3$ are the default objective weights (from metadata)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Implementation in Code:}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp]
// Global cost = weighted combination
// Formula: CTC * w_cost + TotalTime * w_time + TotalPenalty
sol.globalCost = (sol.totalMoneyCost * gCtx.wCost) + 
                 (totalTime * gCtx.wTime) + 
                 sol.totalPenaltyCost;
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Problem Classification and Complexity}
The Velora problem is formally a variant of the \textbf{Heterogeneous Vehicle Routing Problem with Soft Time Windows (HVRPSTW)}, itself a generalization of the classical VRPTW introduced by Solomon \cite{solomon1987}. Key complexity factors:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NP-Hardness}: The classical VRPTW is NP-hard \cite{solomon1987}; our variant with heterogeneous fleet, precedence constraints, and multiple soft penalties is strictly harder \cite{feillet2004}
    \item \textbf{Solution Space}: For $n$ requests and $m$ vehicles, the search space grows as $O(n! \cdot m^n)$
    \item \textbf{Constraint Interaction}: Time feasibility, capacity, and preference constraints interact non-linearly, creating a highly constrained landscape \cite{cordeau2002}
\end{itemize}

This complexity motivates our choice of metaheuristic methods over exact optimization, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:heuristics}.

\section{Optimization}

This section presents a mathematical formulation of the Velora Mobility Optimization problem. The formulation is provided for problem specification and theoretical clarity; the implemented solver relies on heuristic and meta-heuristic methods due to the problem’s computational complexity.

\subsection{Problem Structure and Graph Model}

We model the system as a directed graph $G = (V, A)$, where:
\[
V = D \;\cup\; P \;\cup\; \{c\}.
\]

Here:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $D = \{d_1, d_2, \dots, d_m\}$ is the set of vehicle start locations (depots),
    \item $P = \{1, 2, \dots, n\}$ is the set of employee pickup locations,
    \item $c$ is a \emph{single common drop-off location} (office/campus).
\end{itemize}

Each employee request $i \in P$ consists of a pickup at node $i$ and a drop-off at node $c$.

Let $K$ denote the set of heterogeneous vehicles \cite{golden2008,dondo2007}. Each vehicle $k \in K$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item starts from a depot $d(k) \in D$,
    \item has capacity $Q_k$,
    \item incurs cost $C_k$ per kilometer,
    \item is available from time $A_k$.
\end{itemize}

For each arc $(u,v) \in A$, let:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $d_{uv}$ be the travel distance,
    \item $\tau_{uv} = d_{uv}/v$ be the travel time (constant average speed assumed).
\end{itemize}

Each employee $i$ has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a soft time window $[E_i, L_i]$ for arrival at $c$,
    \item a priority level $P_i \in \{1,2,3,4,5\}$ (lower value = higher priority).
\end{itemize}

---

\subsection{Decision Variables}

For modelling purposes, we define the following decision variables:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $x_{uv}^k \in \{0,1\}$: equals 1 if vehicle $k$ traverses arc $(u,v)$,
    \item $t_u^k \ge 0$: arrival time of vehicle $k$ at node $u$,
    \item $q_u^k \ge 0$: number of passengers onboard vehicle $k$ after leaving node $u$.
\end{itemize}

---

\subsection{Objective Function}

The objective is to minimize total operational cost plus penalty for late arrivals and unassigned employees:

\begin{equation}
\min \; Z = Z_{\text{dist}} + Z_{\text{penalty}} + Z_{\text{unassigned}}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Operating Cost}
\begin{equation}
Z_{\text{dist}} =
\sum_{k \in K} \sum_{(u,v)\in A}
C_k \, d_{uv} \, x_{uv}^k.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Lateness Penalty (Two-Tier Structure)}
For each employee $i$, let $t_c^k$ denote the arrival time at the office (drop-off node $c$) by their assigned vehicle $k$. Let $f(P_i)$ denote the priority-based tolerance (grace period) in minutes. The penalty is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Penalty}_i = 
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } t_c^k \leq L_i + f(P_i) \quad \text{(on-time or within tolerance)} \\[0.3cm]
(t_c^k - L_i - f(P_i)) \cdot M \cdot w(P_i) & \text{if } t_c^k > L_i + f(P_i) \quad \text{(exceeds tolerance)}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $L_i$ is the latest acceptable drop-off time for employee $i$
    \item $f(P_i)$ is the priority-based tolerance: $f(1)=5$, $f(2)=10$, $f(3)=15$, $f(4)=20$, $f(5)=30$ minutes
    \item $M = 1000.0$ is the severe violation penalty multiplier
    \item $w(P_i) = (6 - P_i)$ is the priority weight (higher priority $\Rightarrow$ higher penalty)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key insight:} There is \textbf{no penalty} as long as the employee arrives within $L_i + f(P_i)$. Only arrivals \textit{exceeding} this tolerance deadline incur a severe penalty.

The total lateness penalty is:
\begin{equation}
Z_{\text{penalty}} = \sum_{i\in P} \text{Penalty}_i.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Unassigned Penalty}
Employees not served by any vehicle incur a large penalty:
\begin{equation}
Z_{\text{unassigned}} = M \cdot |\{i \in P : i \text{ unassigned}\}|,\qquad M \gg 1.
\end{equation}

---

\subsection{Constraints}

\subsubsection{Pickup Assignment}
Each employee is served by at most one vehicle:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{k\in K}\sum_{v\in V} x_{iv}^k \le 1
\qquad \forall i \in P.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Flow Conservation}
For each vehicle $k$:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{v\in V} x_{uv}^k - \sum_{v\in V} x_{vu}^k = 0
\qquad \forall u \in V \setminus \{d(k), c\}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Route Structure}
Each vehicle:
\begin{itemize}
    \item starts from exactly one depot $d(k)$,
    \item ends at the common drop node $c$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Capacity Constraints}
Passenger load evolves as:
\begin{align}
0 \le q_u^k &\le Q_k \qquad \forall u,k, \\
q_v^k &\ge q_u^k + \delta(v) - (1-x_{uv}^k)M',
\end{align}
where $\delta(v)=1$ if $v$ is a pickup node and $\delta(v)=0$ otherwise.

\subsubsection{Time Propagation}
\begin{equation}
t_v^k \ge t_u^k + \tau_{uv} - (1-x_{uv}^k)M''
\qquad \forall (u,v)\in A.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Vehicle Availability}
\begin{equation}
t_{d(k)}^k \ge A_k \qquad \forall k\in K.
\end{equation}

---

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

Even under simplified assumptions, the problem is a variant of the Heterogeneous Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows, which is NP-hard \cite{toth2014,golden2008}. The presence of multiple depots, soft time windows, and priority-weighted penalties further increases solution complexity \cite{ismail2021}. Consequently, exact solution approaches are impractical for realistic problem sizes, motivating the heuristic and meta-heuristic framework adopted in this work \cite{laporte2009}.

\section{Heuristics and Meta-Heuristics}
\label{sec:heuristics}

This section discusses standard algorithms used in VRP/VRPTW research and explains why the project uses greedy insertion + Simulated Annealing.

\subsection{Exact Methods (MIP / Branch-and-Cut / Column Generation)}
Exact optimization typically models the problem as a set partitioning/covering formulation with route variables, solved via column generation, branching, and cutting planes \cite{feillet2004,dondo2007,coelho2014}. Theoretical advantage: an optimality certificate. Practical limitation: side constraints (preferences, sharing limits, precedence, soft penalties) complicate pricing and constraints, and runtime can grow rapidly beyond tens of requests.

\subsection{Classical Heuristics (Fast Construction)}
\subsubsection{Savings (Clarke--Wright)}
Savings heuristics merge individual routes based on savings $s_{ij} = d_{0i} + d_{0j} - d_{ij}$ \cite{clarke1964}. They are efficient and often good for capacitated VRP, but incorporating precedence and time windows requires careful merge feasibility checks and often post-repair.

\subsubsection{Sweep / Cluster-first route-second}
Clustering reduces the problem to smaller subproblems \cite{leksakul2017}, but can produce poor results when time windows dominate feasibility (clusters are geometric, feasibility is temporal).

\subsubsection{Insertion Heuristics (Solomon I1/I2)}
Insertion heuristics build routes by inserting requests into partial routes using marginal costs and penalty-aware feasibility. They are well-suited for VRPTW because time feasibility can be checked at insertion time \cite{solomon1987}.

\subsection{Local Search Neighborhoods}
Typical neighborhoods include relocate, exchange, 2-opt, Or-opt, and intra/inter-route shifts. For pickup-and-delivery, the key theoretical issue is that many moves are not closed under feasibility: a move can violate precedence. Therefore, either precedence-preserving operators are required or explicit feasibility checks and rejection/repair must be used.

\subsection{Tabu Search (TS)}
Tabu Search maintains a short-term memory (tabu list) of recently applied moves/attributes to prevent cycling, plus aspiration rules to accept tabu moves if they improve the best-known solution. TS is strong for VRPTW and soft time windows \cite{cordeau2002,taillard1997}, but engineering complexity is higher:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Design of tabu attributes and tenure,
    \item fast incremental cost/feasibility evaluation,
    \item explicit diversification/intensification strategies.
\end{itemize}
For this project’s constraints and timeline, SA provides a simpler stochastic escape mechanism with fewer moving parts.

\subsection{Genetic / Evolutionary Algorithms (GA/EA)}
GA optimizes via population-based evolution \cite{vidal2013}. The main theoretical challenge in constrained VRPTW is that crossover tends to destroy feasibility (time windows, precedence), requiring specialized encodings and repair operators. High-quality GA for VRPTW is typically hybridized with local search (memetic algorithms) \cite{bideq2025}, increasing implementation scope.

\subsection{Swarm/Probabilistic Construction (ACO, PSO)}
ACO constructs solutions probabilistically guided by pheromone trails; PSO adapts continuous update dynamics to combinatorial encodings. These can work but often require many iterations and expensive feasibility checks in constrained VRPTW settings.

\subsection{Large Neighborhood Search (LNS/ALNS), VNS, and Modern Hybrid Solvers}
ALNS iteratively destroys and repairs parts of a solution with adaptive operator weights \cite{ropke2006,shaw1998}; it is among the best general-purpose VRP heuristics when engineered well \cite{demir2012}. However, it requires careful design of destroy/repair operators and parameter tuning, which can be heavy for a short project.

\subsection{Why Greedy Insertion + SA was used (and why others were not)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility-by-construction}: insertion can evaluate feasibility (capacity, precedence, and time) at the moment of assignment.
    \item \textbf{Penalty-compatible energy}: SA naturally operates on the same scalar objective used for evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Controlled exploration}: SA’s Metropolis acceptance provides a theoretically grounded way to accept uphill moves early \cite{kirkpatrick1983}.
    \item \textbf{Implementation tractability}: compared to TS/GA/ALNS, the chosen approach achieves strong savings with fewer specialized components.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Work: HVRPSTW and Employee Shuttle Routing (Selective, Organized)}
This subsection synthesizes the main algorithmic context contained in \texttt{algocontext.txt} into a research-paper style related-work summary. The goal is not to exhaustively list every paper, but to organize representative approaches by methodological family and highlight trade-offs relevant to Velora.

\subsubsection{Exact and Mathematical Programming}
For small-to-medium instances, rich HVRP variants with soft time windows are often formulated as MILPs and solved via branch-and-bound/branch-and-cut. A second exact line is branch-and-price (column generation) which uses a route-based set partitioning model and solves a pricing subproblem to generate candidate routes. These approaches provide optimality certificates but can become computationally heavy when many side constraints are added (heterogeneous eligibility rules, precedence constraints, and multiple penalty terms).

Representative examples include exact formulations for heterogeneous time windows solved using branch-and-price \cite{mutzel2022} and case-study-style MILP solutions for heterogeneous soft-window settings \cite{ismail2021}.

\subsubsection{Construction Heuristics with Tabu/VND}
In real operational contexts (e.g., employee shuttles), classical constructive heuristics (savings, insertion) are frequently paired with tabu search or variable neighborhood descent. The constructive stage produces a feasible schedule quickly; tabu/VND then performs iterative improvement using move neighborhoods (relocate, exchange, 2-opt variants) while managing soft window penalties and feasibility.

In employee transport variants, tabu-search style approaches have been reported to deliver tangible fuel/cost reductions relative to manual scheduling \cite{purba2020}. In heterogeneous VRPTW variants with limited resources, insertion + VND+tabu hybrids provide a pragmatic balance between feasibility and quality \cite{molina2020}.

\subsubsection{Metaheuristics for Large-Scale Instances (GA/ACO/PSO/VNS/ALNS)}
For larger or richer instances, metaheuristics dominate.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GA/EA}: population-based search; strong when paired with local search, but requires feasibility-aware encodings/repair for precedence and time windows.
    \item \textbf{ACO/Swarm}: probabilistic construction; can be competitive with strong problem-specific heuristics and preprocessing.
    \item \textbf{VNS/ALNS}: systematic neighborhood changes or destroy/repair schemes; among the most effective general-purpose VRP solvers when engineered well.
\end{itemize}

For employee transport at large scale, a common pattern is \emph{cluster-then-route}: clustering creates feasible stops/regions and routing metaheuristics optimize sequences, e.g., clustering + ACO for factory-scale employee bus routing \cite{leksakul2017}. For heterogeneous pickup-and-delivery variants with rich constraints, ALNS is frequently used due to its ability to integrate penalties and complex feasibility into a destroy/repair framework \cite{gasque2022}.

\subsubsection{Matheuristics (Hybrid Exact + Metaheuristic)}
Matheuristics combine metaheuristics to generate high-quality candidate routes/solutions and then solve an exact subproblem (often a set partitioning MILP) to select/assign routes optimally under heterogeneous fleet constraints. This pattern is especially natural when the fleet assignment layer can be separated from route construction.

An example in employee shuttle routing is a two-phase memetic + set-partitioning framework reported as a decision-support approach \cite{bideq2025}.

\subsubsection{Learning-Augmented and Data-Driven Methods}
Recent work explores using learning to guide neighborhood selection or destroy/repair choices (e.g., RL-guided adaptive VNS) for complex time window variants \cite{xu2026}. In practice, even simple data-driven preprocessing such as clustering can substantially reduce the effective problem size and stabilize routing quality in employee mobility contexts \cite{leksakul2017}.

\subsubsection{Dynamic and Uncertainty-Aware Models}
Beyond static routing, employee mobility can involve time-varying demand and uncertain travel times. Representative work also explores dynamic linear programming approaches for inhomogeneous fleets \cite{aydin2025}. More broadly, uncertainty-aware approaches (e.g., robust/fuzzy formulations) are complementary to Velora’s static planning and indicate directions for future extensions.

\section{Algorithm Design (Implemented Hybrid)}

\subsection{Phase 1: Multi-Start Constructive Insertion}
Requests are sorted by priority and time urgency; across multiple restarts the order is perturbed to diversify constructions \cite{solomon1987,cordeau2002}.

For vehicle $k$ with route $R_k=(v_0,\dots,v_m)$ and request $u$, evaluate insertion indices $(i,j)$ with $i<j$ and minimize \cite{solomon1987}:
\begin{equation}
\Delta(u,k,i,j)= w_c\,\Delta\text{CTC}(u,k,i,j)+ w_t\,\Delta\text{Time}(u,k,i,j) + \Delta\text{Penalty}(u,k,i,j).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Phase 2: Simulated Annealing (SA)}
Simulated Annealing is a probabilistic metaheuristic for global optimization \cite{kirkpatrick1983}. Given current solution $S$ and neighbor $S'$, define $\Delta E = Z(S')-Z(S)$. Accept
\begin{equation}
\Pr(\text{accept})=
\begin{cases}
1 & \Delta E<0,\\
\exp(-\Delta E/T) & \Delta E\ge 0.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Cooling schedule (project parameters)}
Geometric cooling is used \cite{kirkpatrick1983}:
\begin{equation}
T_{r+1} = \alpha T_r,\qquad T_0=1000.0,\; \alpha=0.995,\; T_{min}=0.1.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Neighborhood operators (implemented)}
The following neighborhood operators are adapted from classical VRP local search \cite{toth2014,shaw1998}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Relocate}: move a request (pickup+dropoff pair) within/between routes.
    \item \textbf{Exchange}: swap two requests between routes.
    \item \textbf{2-Opt}: reverse a route segment; feasibility is re-validated to preserve precedence.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Route Evaluation Function}

The route evaluation is the most frequently called function; it computes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility}: Checks pickup-before-dropoff precedence and capacity constraints
    \item \textbf{Time Propagation}: Simulates vehicle traversal with correct per-vehicle speed
    \item \textbf{Cost Calculation}: Accumulates distance-based cost and time penalties
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Route evaluation (simplified)]
RouteEvaluation evaluateRoute(const Route& r, 
                              const Vehicle& v, 
                              const vector<Request>& reqs) {
    RouteEvaluation eval;
    
    // Check pickup-before-dropoff precedence
    unordered_set<int> pickedUp;
    for (const auto& stop : r.stops) {
        if (stop.type == DROPOFF && 
            pickedUp.find(stop.reqId) == pickedUp.end()) {
            eval.feasible = false;
            return eval;  // Invalid precedence
        }
        if (stop.type == PICKUP) pickedUp.insert(stop.reqId);
    }
    
    // Time propagation with vehicle-specific speed
    double currentTime = v.availabilityTime;
    Location currentLoc = v.startLoc;
    
    for (const auto& stop : r.stops) {
        double dist = getDistance(currentLoc, stop.loc);
        double travelTime = (dist / v.speed) * 60.0;  // minutes
        eval.totalDist += dist;
        currentTime += travelTime;
        
        // Handle pickup time window
        if (stop.type == PICKUP) {
            const Request& req = reqs[stop.reqId];
            if (currentTime < req.earlyTime) {
                eval.penaltyCost += (req.earlyTime - currentTime) * 2.0;
                currentTime = req.earlyTime;  // Wait
            }
        }
        
        // Handle dropoff lateness penalty (two-tier structure)
        if (stop.type == DROPOFF) {
            const Request& req = reqs[stop.reqId];
            double maxDelay = getMaxDelay(req.priority);
            double toleranceDeadline = req.lateTime + maxDelay;
            
            if (currentTime > toleranceDeadline) {
                // Exceeds tolerance - severe penalty
                double excessLateness = currentTime - toleranceDeadline;
                double priorityWeight = (6 - req.priority);
                eval.penaltyCost += excessLateness * M * priorityWeight;
            }
            // else: within L_i or tolerance window - NO PENALTY
        }
        
        currentTime += SERVICE_TIME;  // 2 minutes per stop
        currentLoc = stop.loc;
    }
    
    eval.moneyCost = eval.totalDist * v.costPerKm;
    eval.totalCost = eval.moneyCost + eval.penaltyCost;
    return eval;
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Distance Calculation Module}

The \texttt{MapDistance} class provides a robust distance abstraction:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Distance calculation with fallback]
class MapDistance {
public:
    double distance(double lat1, double lon1, 
                    double lat2, double lon2) const {
        // Fail-fast: if API previously failed, use fallback
        if (globalDisableExternal || apiKey_.empty()) {
            return haversine(lat1, lon1, lat2, lon2) * 1.4;
        }
        
        // Try external API with 100ms timeout
        // On failure, permanently switch to Haversine
        // ...
    }
    
private:
    double haversine(double lat1, double lon1, 
                     double lat2, double lon2) const {
        const double R = 6371.0;  // Earth radius (km)
        double dLat = toRad(lat2 - lat1);
        double dLon = toRad(lon2 - lon1);
        double a = sin(dLat/2)*sin(dLat/2) + 
                   cos(toRad(lat1))*cos(toRad(lat2)) *
                   sin(dLon/2)*sin(dLon/2);
        return R * 2 * asin(sqrt(a));
    }
};
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Key Design Decision}: We apply a 1.4$\times$ multiplier to Haversine distances to approximate road distance \cite{ichoua2003}. This factor is derived from empirical studies showing that actual road distances in urban areas are typically 30--50\% longer than straight-line distances due to road network geometry.

\subsection{Infeasibility Handling Strategy}
\label{subsec:infeasibility}

A key requirement from the problem statement (confirmed via WhatsApp clarifications) is that \textbf{no employee should be left unassigned}, even if their constraints are physically impossible to satisfy. Our system implements a multi-tier approach:

\textbf{Tier 1: Strict Insertion}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Strict feasibility check]
// Check time window with tolerance
double maxDelay = getMaxDelay(req.priority);
if (arrivalTime > req.lateTime + maxDelay * 2) {
    return false;  // Infeasible
}
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Tier 2: Relaxed Insertion}
If strict insertion fails for all vehicles:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Relaxed insertion attempt]
// Try with relaxed time constraints
auto ins = findBestInsertion(route, reqId, vehicle, reqs, 
                             /* relaxedMode = */ true);
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Tier 3: Forced Assignment}
If even relaxed insertion fails, the system:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Identifies the vehicle with minimum total violation
    \item Forces the assignment regardless of time feasibility
    \item Applies severe penalty ($M_{\text{maxDelay}} = 100,000$ per minute excess)
\end{enumerate}

This ensures 100\% coverage while clearly flagging problematic assignments in the output.

\section{Challenges Faced and Solutions}

This section documents the significant technical challenges encountered during development and the solutions implemented to address them.

\subsection{Challenge 1: Pickup-Dropoff Precedence under Local Search}

\textbf{Problem Description}: 
Standard VRP neighborhood operators like 2-opt reverse route segments, which can inadvertently place a dropoff before its corresponding pickup---a hard constraint violation \cite{parragh2008}. Unlike classical VRP where each customer is visited once, pickup-and-delivery problems require maintaining precedence relationships \cite{psaraftis1980}.

\textbf{Impact}: 
Initial implementation of 2-opt would occasionally produce invalid solutions where employees were ``dropped off'' before being picked up.

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Every candidate move is validated before acceptance
    \item A fast precedence check scans the route order:
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Precedence validation]
bool checkPrecedence(const Route& route) {
    unordered_set<int> pickedUp;
    for (const auto& stop : route.stops) {
        if (stop.type == PICKUP) {
            pickedUp.insert(stop.reqId);
        } else {  // DROPOFF
            if (pickedUp.find(stop.reqId) == pickedUp.end()) {
                return false;  // Dropoff before pickup!
            }
        }
    }
    return true;
}
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Outcome}: All generated solutions now satisfy precedence constraints. Invalid moves are rejected during SA, not after.

\subsection{Challenge 2: Consistency of Distance/Time Calculations}

\textbf{Problem Description}:
The system has multiple modules (Python parser, C++ solver, Python reporter) that need consistent distance calculations. Discrepancies would cause:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Solver computing one travel time, report showing another
    \item ``Impossible'' speeds appearing in reports (e.g., 200 km/h)
    \item Baseline comparisons being unfair
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Impact}:
Early versions showed inconsistent times between solver output and generated reports.

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Single Source of Truth}: The C++ solver computes all distances using the \texttt{MapDistance} class
    \item \textbf{Consistent Factor}: Both C++ and Python use the same road factor ($1.4 \times$ Haversine)
    \item \textbf{Per-Vehicle Speed}: Each vehicle's speed is respected (no global constant)
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption=Matching Python distance calculation]
def haversine_distance(lat1, lon1, lat2, lon2):
    R = 6371.0  # km
    # ... Haversine formula ...
    return R * c * 1.4  # MUST match solver's factor
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Challenge 3: Handling Physically Impossible Requests (TC04)}

\textbf{Problem Description}:
Test Case 4 contains an employee (E10) whose pickup location is approximately 25km from the office, requiring $\sim$45--60 minutes of travel time. However, their time window is only 30 minutes---\textit{physically impossible to satisfy}.

\textbf{Impact}:
A naive implementation would either:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Leave E10 unassigned (violating ``must-go'' policy)
    \item Crash or produce invalid output
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
Three-tier insertion strategy (described in Section~\ref{subsec:infeasibility}):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Try strict insertion $\rightarrow$ Fails
    \item Try relaxed insertion (ignore time window) $\rightarrow$ Succeeds
    \item Apply large penalty and flag in output
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Outcome}: E10 is assigned to V03, arriving at 09:59 (59 minutes late). The penalty cost ($\sim$75,000) dominates the objective, but the employee is transported. The report clearly marks E10 as ``LATE''.

\subsection{Challenge 4: Penalty Dominance and Energy Landscape}

\textbf{Problem Description}:
Large penalty values (e.g., 50,000 for unassigned, 100,000 for max delay violation) create a ``rugged'' energy landscape where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Many local moves have zero improvement (stuck in penalty regions)
    \item SA may not escape local minima
    \item Solution quality depends heavily on initial construction
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High Initial Temperature}: $T_0 = 1000$ allows accepting moves with $\Delta E$ up to several thousand
    \item \textbf{Multi-Start Construction}: 10 random restarts provide diverse initial solutions
    \item \textbf{Slow Cooling}: $\alpha = 0.995$ ensures gradual temperature decrease
    \item \textbf{Early Stopping Protection}: Continue even with high penalties (don't abort)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Challenge 5: Per-Vehicle Speed Handling}

\textbf{Problem Description}:
The original implementation used a global average speed for all vehicles. However, the test cases include heterogeneous vehicles with different speeds (e.g., 2-wheelers at 40 km/h, sedans at 30 km/h, vans at 32 km/h).

\textbf{Impact}:
Using a wrong speed would cause:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Incorrect arrival time predictions
    \item False time window violations
    \item Suboptimal vehicle selection
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
Refactored route evaluation to use per-vehicle speed:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp]
double speedKmph = (v.speed > 0) ? v.speed : DEFAULT_SPEED;
double travelTime = (dist / speedKmph) * 60.0;  // minutes
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Challenge 6: API Reliability and Fallback}

\textbf{Problem Description}:
External maps APIs (Google, OSRM) can be:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Slow (>100ms per call, causing solver timeout)
    \item Unavailable (network issues, rate limits)
    \item Expensive (per-call pricing)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Solution Implemented}:
Circuit breaker pattern with aggressive timeout:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=cpp, caption=Circuit breaker implementation]
// 100ms timeout - if exceeded, disable API globally
curl_easy_setopt(curl, CURLOPT_TIMEOUT_MS, 100L);

if (res != CURLE_OK) {
    globalDisableExternal = true;  // Permanent fallback
    cerr << "[Warning] API failed. Using Haversine * 1.4\n";
}
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Outcome}: System remains fast and reliable regardless of network conditions.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

\subsection{Summary of Achievements}

The Velora Mobility Optimizer successfully addresses the corporate employee transportation optimization problem with the following accomplishments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Effective Cost Reduction}: Achieved 53.8\%--77.0\% cost savings compared to individual ride baselines across all test cases
    
    \item \textbf{Complete Coverage}: All employees are assigned to vehicles, including handling of physically infeasible requests through forced assignment
    
    \item \textbf{Constraint Satisfaction}: Hard constraints (capacity, precedence) are never violated; soft constraints (time windows, preferences) are handled through penalty optimization
    
    \item \textbf{Computational Efficiency}: Sub-second solve times for all test cases (up to 100 requests), suitable for operational deployment
    
    \item \textbf{Robust Implementation}: End-to-end pipeline from Excel input to human-readable reports, with comprehensive error handling
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Technical Contributions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Implementation of Solomon I1-style insertion heuristic \cite{solomon1987} adapted for many-to-one pickup-and-delivery
    \item Simulated Annealing \cite{kirkpatrick1983} with precedence-aware neighborhood operators
    \item Three-tier infeasibility handling strategy \cite{taillard1997}
    \item Robust distance calculation with API fallback
    \item Automated test pipeline for reproducible evaluation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Static Planning}: Current implementation assumes all requests are known in advance; does not handle dynamic arrivals
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Travel Times}: Uses fixed vehicle speeds; no real-time traffic consideration
    \item \textbf{Single Objective}: Multi-objective optimization (Pareto fronts) not implemented \cite{vidal2013}
    \item \textbf{No Learning Component}: Algorithm parameters are fixed; no adaptive tuning based on instance characteristics \cite{nazari2018,kool2019}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}

For the final evaluation and beyond, the following enhancements are planned:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS)}: Implement destroy/repair operators with adaptive selection weights, as recommended in the literature \cite{ropke2006}
    
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Re-optimization}: Handle new requests arriving during the planning horizon \cite{ichoua2003}
    
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Traffic Integration}: Use live traffic data for more accurate travel time estimation
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Objective Optimization}: Implement NSGA-II or similar to generate Pareto-optimal trade-off solutions
    
    \item \textbf{Machine Learning Augmentation} \cite{nazari2018,kool2019,xu2026}: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Clustering for stop consolidation (K-means preprocessing) \cite{leksakul2017}
        \item Learned neighborhood selection (RL-guided search)
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Enhanced Reporting}: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Visualization of routes on maps
        \item Employee-facing schedule notifications
        \item Management dashboard with KPIs
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conclusion}

The Velora Mobility Optimizer demonstrates that hybrid metaheuristic approaches---combining fast constructive heuristics with iterative improvement \cite{cordeau2002,vidal2013}---can effectively solve real-world vehicle routing problems with complex constraints. The system achieves substantial cost savings while maintaining service quality, validating the practical applicability of operations research techniques to corporate transportation optimization \cite{laporte2009,toth2014}.

The modular architecture and comprehensive testing framework provide a solid foundation for future enhancements as we progress toward the final evaluation.
